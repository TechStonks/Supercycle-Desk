# Supercycle-Desk
It's a self-contained dashboard, rebuilt fresh each day from real market data, covering 23 stocks across AI chips, memory, and AI infrastructure/hardware, alongside the broad indices and a few macro reference assets.

What's on the page

Today's Briefing. A short, plain-language summary of the day's market and macro story sits right at the top, so you know why everything moved before you start looking at what moved.

A "data as of" badge. Right under the headline, a small badge tells you whether you're looking at a live intraday quote or a final market close — useful on days I run it mid-session instead of after hours.

Markets Snapshot. A quick strip across the top covering the Dow, Nasdaq, S&P 500, crude oil, gold, the 10-year yield, and bitcoin — each with a same-day sparkline, so you get the shape of the move, not just the number.

Market Trend chart. A full chart of those same indices and assets, with controls to reshape it however you want (more on that below).

Top Movers. The day's and week's biggest gainers and losers across the watchlist, pulled out separately so you don't have to scan the whole table to find them.

The AI Hardware Watchlist. The main event — all 23 tickers with price, day change, and a 52-week range rail, so you can see at a glance how close each stock is sitting to its highs or lows.

Sector Trends. Three separate trend charts — AI Chips, Memory, and AI Infra/Hardware — so you can compare stocks within a sector without index-heavy names or macro assets muddying the comparison.

Market Calendar. FOMC meetings, jobs and inflation reports, and each stock's own earnings and dividend dates, all on one navigable monthly calendar.

How to use it

Light and dark mode. Top-right toggle. It remembers your choice for next time.

Switch a chart between % and $. Both the Market Trend and Sector Trends charts default to a percent view, indexed to 100 at the start of the window — that's what lets you compare a $20 stock against a $2,000 one on equal footing. Click $ to flip to real prices; in that mode, the chip row underneath becomes a single-stock switcher rather than a multi-select, since overlaying several raw price series on one axis gets unreadable fast.

Series chips. Under each chart is a row of small pills, one per stock or asset, color-coded to match the lines. Click one to hide or show that line individually. Click All to toggle everything at once — it's a genuine on/off switch: if every name is currently showing, one click clears the whole chart, and one more click brings everything back.

Range tabs. 1D, 1W, 1M, 3M, YTD, 1Y, 5Y — appears under the chip row once you're in $ mode, letting you zoom into a single trading session or pull all the way out to a multi-year view.

Expand a chart. Click the small arrows icon in the corner of either chart card to pop it into a larger, full-width view — handy once you're looking at years of daily data and want more room to actually read it.

Filter and sort the watchlist. Search by ticker or company name, filter by sector (AI Chips / Memory / AI Infra), or use the quick filters for Gainers/Losers and Near High/Near Low. Click any column header to sort the table by it.

Click a row to open it. Every stock in the watchlist expands into a detail view with:

Volume and market cap
The country the company is headquartered in
A small earnings-history chart — one solid dot per quarter for the actual reported EPS, one lighter dot for what analysts had estimated going in, so you can see at a glance whether the company's been beating or missing, and by how much
My own notes on that day's catalyst, when there is one

The Market Calendar. Click any date to see what's scheduled — a small colored dot tells you the event type (Fed decision, jobs report, CPI, PPI, earnings, or dividend) before you even click in.
